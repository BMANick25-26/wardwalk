What is a ward walk?

Purpose

A ward walk is a structured way to engage with doctors in your local hospital. You walk around the hospital and across wards, speak to doctors where they work, and try to reach people who do not usually engage with the BMA or with reps.

You spend time chatting, deliver key messages, listen to issues, and answer questions where you can.

What good looks like

  • You reach doctors who do not normally engage.
  • You hear clear themes, not just one-off complaints.
  • People leave knowing how to contact you and what will happen next.
  • You do not overpromise. You take issues away and follow up properly.

Timing

  • Plan for around 1–2 hours total, depending on hospital size.
  • Aim for 15–20 minutes per ward where you can.
  • If a ward is too busy, move on and return later.

What it is not

  • Not a confrontation with management.
  • Not an investigation or a place to collect names.
  • Not a promise that every issue will be fixed immediately.

How to prep

Purpose

Preparation is about walking onto the ward with a plan. You should know which wards you are aiming for, what your main focus is, and how you will capture themes and follow up.

What good preparation looks like

  • Aim to visit all wards over time.
  • If you have time constraints, prioritise key wards you have not visited recently or wards with poorer engagement.
  • Have a clear theme for the walk (pay, training, rota, workload).
  • Bring what you need to follow up: QR code/link, contact details, and a way to note themes.

Common pitfalls

  • Trying to cover too many topics in one walk.
  • Turning the walk into a long debate instead of listening.
  • Forgetting key materials and missing the chance to signpost or follow up.
  • Overpromising outcomes.

10-minute prep model

  1. Select wards: plan to cover all wards over time. If short on time, target low-engagement or not-recently-visited wards.
  2. Define your focus: choose one theme so conversations stay coherent.
  3. Prepare questions: 3–5 open prompts that encourage real answers.
  4. Gather materials: QR/contact details, campaign materials if needed, and a way to note themes.
  5. Know escalation routes: be clear where issues go after the walk.

What to do during

  1. Introduce yourself and your role
  2. Check it’s a good time
  3. Listen for themes
  4. Note patterns, not names
  5. Offer next steps
  6. Invite follow-up

What to do post ward walk

Triage

  • Urgent safety issues
  • Important recurring themes
  • Local quick wins

Close the loop

  • Update staff
  • Track actions
  • Escalate appropriately

Pizza & Pay

Planning

  • Room, time, permissions
