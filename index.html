How to do a ward walk

What is a ward walk?

Purpose

A ward walk is a structured way to engage with doctors in your local hospital. You walk around the hospital and across wards, speak to doctors where they work, and try to reach people who do not usually engage with the BMA or with reps.

You spend time chatting, deliver key messages, listen to issues, and answer questions where you can.

Why ward walks matter

  • Ward walks let us hear directly from doctors about their working lives, in their own environment.
  • They help spot recurring problems early, before issues escalate.
  • They build visibility and trust, especially with doctors who do not engage via emails or WhatsApp.
  • You do not need to be a BMA rep to ward walk — just someone who wants to improve pay and working conditions.

What good looks like

A good ward walk leaves doctors feeling listened to and taken seriously. Even when there is no immediate solution, people should feel clearer about what support exists and what will happen next.

  • You reach doctors who do not normally engage.
  • You hear clear themes, not just one-off complaints.
  • People leave knowing how to contact you and what will happen next.
  • You do not overpromise. You take issues away and follow up properly.
What ward walks are not
Ward walks are not about telling doctors what they should think or do. They are not about making promises or having all the answers. Listening, understanding concerns, and signposting are the priority.

Common pitfalls to avoid

  • Feeling pressure to have all the answers.
  • Trying to resolve issues immediately on the ward.
  • Avoiding busy or disengaged wards.
  • Letting conversations turn into venting rather than calm, supportive discussion.
  • Failing to note recurring themes and feed them back.

Timing

  • Plan for around 1–2 hours total, depending on hospital size.
  • Aim for 15–20 minutes per ward where you can.
  • If a ward is too busy, move on and return later.

How to prep

Purpose

Preparation is about walking onto the ward with a plan. You should know which wards you are aiming for, what your main focus is, and how you will capture themes and follow up.

What good preparation looks like

  • Aim to visit all wards over time.
  • If you have time constraints, prioritise key wards you have not visited recently or wards with poorer engagement.
  • Have a clear theme for the walk (pay, training, rota, workload).
  • Bring what you need to follow up: QR code/link, contact details, and a way to note themes.

Common pitfalls

  • Trying to cover too many topics in one walk.
  • Turning the walk into a long debate instead of listening.
  • Forgetting key materials and missing the chance to signpost or follow up.
  • Overpromising outcomes.
  • Feeling pressure to have all the answers.
  • Trying to resolve issues immediately on the ward.
  • Avoiding busy or disengaged wards.
  • Letting conversations turn into venting rather than calm, supportive discussion.
  • Failing to note recurring themes and feed them back.

10-minute prep model

  1. Select wards: plan to cover all wards over time. If short on time, target low-engagement or not-recently-visited wards.
  2. Define your focus: choose one theme so conversations stay coherent.
  3. Prepare questions: 3–5 open prompts that encourage real answers.
  4. Gather materials: QR/contact details, campaign materials if needed, and a way to note themes.
  5. Know escalation routes: be clear where issues go after the walk.

What to do during the ward walk

Your job on the ward walk is simple. Reach doctors you do not normally hear from. Share the headlines. Listen properly. Capture issues and follow up.

1. Choose the right wards first

  • Start with wards that are underrepresented or have low engagement.
  • Prioritise areas you do not hear from often.
  • Assume low engagement can mean unmet problems, not lack of interest.
  • If time is tight, do fewer wards properly rather than rushing every ward.

2. Introduce yourself clearly

  • Say your name and that you are a BMA representative.
  • Be explicit about why you are there today.
  • Keep it short. Doctors decide in the first 10 seconds if they can engage.
Example opener
“Hi, I’m ...... I’m one of the local BMA reps. Is now a good time for a quick chat?”

3. Always check if it’s a good time

  • Ask first. Do not assume you can take their time.
  • If they are busy, do not push.
  • Say you will come back later, then actually do it.
If they’re too busy
“No problem at all. I’ll come back later when things are calmer.”

4. Give the headlines

  • Know the key national and local messages before you start.
  • Keep it to 2–3 points. Do not lecture.
  • Use plain language and avoid jargon.
Headlines structure
National update → Local impact → What you want them to do next

5. Listen and answer questions

  • Ask one open question and let them speak.
  • Listen for themes: rota gaps, training access, exception reporting, study leave.
  • Answer what you can. Do not guess.
It’s fine to say you don’t know
“I don’t know the answer to that right now. If you’re happy, I’ll take your number and come back to you.”

6. Capture details for follow-up

  • Only take contact details if you genuinely intend to follow up.
  • Be clear what you will do with the information.
  • If you are escalating via the LNC or IRO, say so.
  • Write down the issue as a theme, not a long story.
Minimum follow-up note
Ward → Issue theme → Who it needs escalating to → Contact (if needed)

7. Signpost: keep people connected

  • Encourage doctors to join the relevant local strike and coordination groups.
  • This keeps them up to date with national information and local actions.
  • Make it easy: QR codes, Linktree, or a direct message.

8. Repeat the loop until you’ve covered the hospital

  • Move on. Do not get stuck on one ward.
  • Do a second pass if you promised to return.
  • Keep going until you have covered every ward you planned for.

Example talk track

“Hi, my name’s ......... I’m a BMA rep. I’m here today to talk about the current ballot.

The BMA is balloting to extend our strike mandate so we can continue negotiating with the government.

We’re uniting the profession behind a simple message: jobs and pay.

We’re asking you to vote yes, and to return your ballot as quickly as you can.

Is there anything on this ward you think the BMA needs to hear about right now?”

Keep it short. Then listen.

How conversations move people

  • People are persuaded by clear, consistent messages repeated over time.
  • Doctors are more likely to listen to colleagues they know and trust.
  • Use multiple routes: in-person, WhatsApp, posters, follow-up messages.
  • You are not trying to win every argument — moving someone “a bit” is success.

What to do after the ward walk

A good ward walk does not end when you leave the ward. What you do afterwards is what builds trust, fixes problems, and makes the next ward walk easier.

1. Pause and reset

  • Grab a cup of tea or water.
  • Ward walks involve a lot of talking and listening.
  • You deserve a short break before moving on to follow-up tasks.

2. Let us know you’ve completed a ward walk

  • Tell us that you have completed a ward walk.
  • This helps track activity and identify where support is needed.
Action
Complete the ward walk feedback form.

3. Escalate urgent safety issues

  • If any urgent patient safety or workload concerns came up, act on them promptly.
  • Contact your Trust’s Guardian of Safe Working, usually by email.
  • Do not sit on urgent safety information.
Rule of thumb
If it affects safety today, escalate today.

4. Document recurring themes

  • Write down the key issues that came up repeatedly.
  • Focus on themes rather than individual stories.
  • These themes form the baseline for improvement and future ward walks.
Examples of themes
Rota gaps, exception reporting problems, training access, study leave issues

5. Look for quick local wins

  • Some issues can be resolved quickly with the right conversation or email.
  • This might include rota clarifications or raising issues with the Guardian of Safe Working.
  • Small fixes build confidence and credibility.

6. Contact your regional reps if needed

  • If anything complex or sensitive comes up, do not handle it alone.
  • Your regional reps are there to support you.
  • They are always happy to be contacted for advice or escalation.

7. Close the loop

  • If you said you would follow something up, make sure you do.
  • Update staff on what action has been taken.
  • Closing the loop builds trust and encourages future engagement.
Close the loop means
Action taken → feedback given → expectation set

8. Keep the IRO informed

  • If you have asked staff to complete a web form for a specific issue, let your IRO know.
  • This allows them to watch for submissions and act promptly.

If you’re unsure or worried

  • If you are uncertain about anything that came up, ask for advice.
  • A quick phone call with a regional rep can prevent problems later.
  • You are not expected to handle everything alone.

Pizza & Pay

A Pizza & Pay event is a short, informal way to reach a large number of doctors at once. The aim is to share key updates, listen to concerns, and signpost support without taking people away from clinical work for longer than necessary.

1. Coordination

  • Link in early with your local:
    LNC representative
    Industrial Relations Officer (IRO).
    Regional reps
  • Confirm availability, support, and budget arrangements (this MUST go through your IRO or region Chair.
  • Clarify any local considerations early.

2. Planning essentials

Venue and timing

  • Secure a room with enough seating and space for food (doctors’ messes usually work well).
  • Choose a time that maximises attendance:
    • Lunchtime
    • Pre-teaching or post-handover slots
  • Check any local permissions needed for food delivery.

Budget and dietary needs

  • Confirm the budget with the BMA or IRO.
  • Order food with clear dietary options (for example vegetarian).
  • Include drinks, napkins, and plates to avoid mess and disruption.

3. Promotion

  • Posters in the mess.
  • WhatsApp groups and rota groups.
  • Email via the LNC representative.

Messaging should be short, clear, and direct.

4. Short talk track (5–7 minutes)

  • Brief national update on pay, jobs, or other relevant issues.
  • Local issues such as rota gaps, exception reporting, study leave, or training access.
  • What the BMA is doing now.
  • What is coming next.

5. Listening section

  • Open the floor for questions and concerns.
  • Listen for themes rather than individual cases.
  • Capture issues for escalation via the LNC or IRO.
  • Signpost to local reps, membership support, and employment advice routes.

6. Next steps

  • Explain how doctors can stay involved (for example strike groups, ward walks, encouraging ballot returns).
  • Share contact details for local representatives.
  • Ensure BMA materials are available:
    • Lanyards
    • Posters
    • Pens
    These can be ordered via the BMA website.

7. Format and timing

  • Aim for around one hour in total.
  • Be mindful that doctors will need to return to clinical areas promptly.
  • Ending on time builds trust and encourages future engagement.
