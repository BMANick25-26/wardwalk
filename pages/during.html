What to do during the ward walk

Your job on the ward walk is simple. Reach doctors you do not normally hear from. Share the headlines. Listen properly. Capture issues and follow up.

1. Choose the right wards first

  • Start with wards that are underrepresented or have low engagement.
  • Prioritise areas you do not hear from often.
  • Assume low engagement can mean unmet problems, not lack of interest.
  • If time is tight, do fewer wards properly rather than rushing every ward.

2. Introduce yourself clearly

  • Say your name and that you are a BMA representative.
  • Be explicit about why you are there today.
  • Keep it short. Doctors decide in the first 10 seconds if they can engage.
Example opener
“Hi, I’m ...... I’m one of the local BMA reps. Is now a good time for a quick chat?”

3. Always check if it’s a good time

  • Ask first. Do not assume you can take their time.
  • If they are busy, do not push.
  • Say you will come back later, then actually do it.
If they’re too busy
“No problem at all. I’ll come back later when things are calmer.”

4. Give the headlines

  • Know the key national and local messages before you start.
  • Keep it to 2–3 points. Do not lecture.
  • Use plain language and avoid jargon.
Headlines structure
National update → Local impact → What you want them to do next

5. Listen and answer questions

  • Ask one open question and let them speak.
  • Listen for themes: rota gaps, training access, exception reporting, study leave.
  • Answer what you can. Do not guess.
It’s fine to say you don’t know
“I don’t know the answer to that right now. If you’re happy, I’ll take your number and come back to you.”

6. Capture details for follow-up

  • Only take contact details if you genuinely intend to follow up.
  • Be clear what you will do with the information.
  • If you are escalating via the LNC or IRO, say so.
  • Write down the issue as a theme, not a long story.
Minimum follow-up note
Ward → Issue theme → Who it needs escalating to → Contact (if needed)

7. Signpost: keep people connected

  • Encourage doctors to join the relevant local strike and coordination groups.
  • This keeps them up to date with national information and local actions.
  • Make it easy: QR codes, Linktree, or a direct message.

8. Repeat the loop until you’ve covered the hospital

  • Move on. Do not get stuck on one ward.
  • Do a second pass if you promised to return.
  • Keep going until you have covered every ward you planned for.

Example talk track

“Hi, my name’s ......... I’m a BMA rep. I’m here today to talk about the current ballot.

The BMA is balloting to extend our strike mandate so we can continue negotiating with the government.

We’re uniting the profession behind a simple message: jobs and pay.

We’re asking you to vote yes, and to return your ballot as quickly as you can.

Is there anything on this ward you think the BMA needs to hear about right now?”

Keep it short. Then listen.

How conversations move people

  • People are persuaded by clear, consistent messages repeated over time.
  • Doctors are more likely to listen to colleagues they know and trust.
  • Use multiple routes: in-person, WhatsApp, posters, follow-up messages.
  • You are not trying to win every argument — moving someone “a bit” is success.
